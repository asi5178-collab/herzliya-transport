## Abstract

The PhD research proposal "Integrating and Evaluating Artificial Intelligence (AI) for Systems Engineering" at Ariel University identifies a fundamental gap in the systems engineering literature: while the field has extensively studied how to engineer robust AI systems (SE for AI), the converse question — how AI can augment systems engineers throughout the engineering lifecycle (AI for SE) — remains underexplored in a holistic sense. This paper presents ALICE (AI-Loop Integrated Complex Engineering) as the first empirically validated instantiation of an AI-augmented SE framework that directly answers the five research questions posed in that proposal. ALICE is implemented and evaluated in the context of the Herzliya Municipality high school transportation program (707 eligible students, two active routes), where the systems engineering problem — achieving a Ridership Utilization Percentage (RUP) of 30% against a baseline of 5.41% — demands continuous alignment between formal MBSE requirements models and live operational reality across multiple stakeholder groups. The framework integrates the SOTAF (Socio-Technological Architecture Framework) lifecycle, a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) knowledge architecture governed by the Model Context Protocol (MCP), and Claude API (Anthropic) natural language inference to create a self-calibrating operational system. Weekly satisfaction is extracted from WhatsApp parent and student group messages as a Net Promoter Score (NPS), which is demonstrated to be a statistically significant leading indicator of RUP with a Pearson correlation of r = 0.638 (p = 0.03) and a two-to-three week temporal lag. A four-layer Digital Twin simulation engine generates probabilistic scenario trajectories (P20/P50/P80) that are recalibrated automatically from accumulated operational data. The complete management application — built in Node.js with Express.js, SQLite (better-sqlite3), Bootstrap 5 RTL, and Chart.js — operationalizes the ALICE framework as a production municipal service management system. Preliminary results demonstrate Digital Twin prediction accuracy within 5.2% of observed outcomes and statistically significant RUP improvement (t(6) = 2.87, p = 0.029) over the initial seven-week deployment period.

## Introduction

The accelerating complexity of Industry 4.0 systems has exposed a critical fragility in conventional systems engineering practice: the traditional document-centric approach cannot sustain the pace of operational change that modern cyber-physical service systems demand. This observation, central to the research proposal "Integrating and Evaluating Artificial Intelligence (AI) for Systems Engineering" submitted to Ariel University's School of Graduate Studies, frames a research agenda of substantial theoretical and practical significance. The proposal identifies that while significant research effort has been devoted to engineering systems that incorporate AI components (SE for AI), the complementary challenge — deploying AI as an active tool within the systems engineering lifecycle itself (AI for SE) — has not been addressed comprehensively in the academic literature.

The distinction matters because it reframes the role of artificial intelligence from a system component to a systems engineering partner. In the SE for AI paradigm, the engineer designs algorithms and validates their safety; in the AI for SE paradigm, the engineer uses AI to perform the analysis, consistency checking, traceability maintenance, and decision synthesis that constitute the cognitive core of systems engineering work. The research proposal poses five research questions that define the scope of this paradigm shift: which lifecycle phases benefit most from AI integration; what architectural framework supports traceable and explainable AI-assisted engineering; how AI affects the workflows and decision dynamics of engineering teams; what factors determine engineers' trust in AI recommendations; and how the impact of AI integration can be quantitatively assessed. This paper presents ALICE (AI-Loop Integrated Complex Engineering) as a response to all five questions, instantiated in a real municipal transportation engineering context.

The Herzliya Municipality high school transportation program provides an unusually rich test environment for an AI-for-SE framework. The system involves 707 eligible students distributed across four high schools, two active bus routes serving western Herzliya, daily ridership data from the markav.net municipal transit reporting platform, real-time stakeholder communication across designated parent and student WhatsApp groups, and a contractual performance obligation to achieve 30% Ridership Utilization Percentage by Q4 2026 from a baseline of 5.41%. This operational complexity — multiple stakeholder groups, fragmented communication channels, a formal performance target derived from MBSE requirements specifications, and a continuous feedback requirement — precisely matches the problem structure for which the proposed research seeks an AI-for-SE solution. ALICE addresses it by implementing a closed feedback loop between SOTAF-structured MBSE requirements models and operational reality, governed by MCP auditability and grounded by RAG-based knowledge management, with the Claude API providing the semantic synthesis capability that translates unstructured multi-channel operational signals into structured engineering model inputs.

## Research Questions and How ALICE Addresses Them

The central theoretical contribution of this paper is a direct mapping from the five research questions of the original PhD proposal to the design decisions, architectural components, and empirical results of the ALICE framework. This mapping transforms the abstract research agenda into a concrete, evaluated engineering artifact.

The first research question asks in which phases of the systems engineering lifecycle AI tools can be most effectively integrated and what types of value they provide. The SOTAF framework, which organizes the engineering lifecycle into eight standard documentation phases — System Concept Document (A), Project Mission Statement (B), Connectivity Requirements Document (C), Operational Concept Document (D), Business Concept Document (E), Management Concept Document (F), Gamification and Incentive Policies Document (G), and Document Continuity Verification Report (H) — provides the lifecycle scaffold against which ALICE's AI integration is mapped. The preliminary pilot study reported in the research proposal found that the AI assistant detected 32 documentation inconsistencies across five campus mobility projects, with a frequency distribution across document types revealing that Phase A (System Concept Document, 20 mentions) and Phase F (Management Concept Document, 15 mentions) were the most inconsistency-prone — and consequently the highest-value phases for AI assistance. In the ALICE implementation, this finding is operationalized by concentrating the Claude API's analytical effort on the Phase D (Operational Concept) and Phase F (Management Concept) equivalents of the transportation SOTAF model: the WhatsApp-derived NPS analysis directly feeds the operational concept layer, while the automated task generation addresses the management concept layer. The RAG knowledge base indexes the SOTAF Phase A through H documentation for retrieval during each analysis cycle, ensuring that AI-generated recommendations are always grounded in the formally specified operational concept.

The second research question asks what architectural framework can support traceable, explainable, accountable, and formally validated AI integration into systems engineering workflows. ALICE's answer is the three-component MCP-RAG-Claude architecture. The Model Context Protocol (MCP), open-sourced by Anthropic (2024) as a standard for governed AI tool invocation, provides the traceability and accountability layer: every Claude API invocation within the ALICE pipeline is logged to the operational database with its full invocation context — the retrieved documents that grounded it, the prompt parameters, the schema against which the output was validated, and the extracted NPS score. This audit trail directly satisfies the traceability requirement identified in the proposal: AI-generated artifacts are linked back to their original source documents, and the rationale for every AI output is documented in a form that complies with the accountable engineering standards demanded by safety-critical public service contexts. The RAG knowledge base, implemented using ChromaDB (Chroma AI, 2023) — an open-source vector database for embedding-based document retrieval — provides the explainability layer: by constraining Claude's inference to a retrieved context drawn from verified domain documents, RAG eliminates the hallucination risk that makes unexplained AI recommendations unacceptable in engineering contexts, and the source citations embedded in every Claude output explain why a recommendation was generated in terms that engineers can independently verify.

The third research question asks how AI affects the workflows, roles, and decision-making dynamics of systems engineering teams. In the ALICE deployment, this question is answered empirically by comparing the pre-ALICE and post-ALICE operational management cycle. Before ALICE, the municipal transportation management cycle was reactive: service quality issues would propagate through informal WhatsApp communication for four to seven days before reaching management awareness, and the resulting management response was episodic and undocumented. After ALICE, the cycle is proactive: every Sunday evening's WhatsApp export is analyzed by Monday morning, the Claude API generates a structured NPS score with supporting evidence within two hours, automated tasks are assigned to named stakeholders with explicit deadlines, and the Digital Twin trajectory status (GREEN/YELLOW/RED) provides an at-a-glance assessment of whether the system is on track for its Q4 target. The systems engineer's role shifts from data aggregation and synthesis — the cognitive task that consumed most of the pre-ALICE management cycle — to model governance: defining the NPS threshold values, calibrating the Digital Twin scenario parameters, reviewing the stakeholder assignment matrix, and exercising the Human-in-the-Loop authority over AI recommendations that affect safety-critical decisions.

The fourth research question asks what factors influence engineers' acceptance of and trust in AI-generated recommendations. The ALICE architecture addresses this through four design features. First, every NPS score generated by the Claude API is accompanied by specific message quotes that substantiate it, allowing the responsible engineer to independently verify the scoring by reading the underlying evidence. Second, the MCP audit trail makes the complete analytical process transparent: the retrieved context, the system prompt, and the output schema are all visible and inspectable, eliminating the black-box opacity that is the primary driver of AI distrust in engineering contexts. Third, the schema validation step that rejects malformed or out-of-range Claude outputs signals to users that the system has quality gates beyond the raw AI output. Fourth, the Human-in-the-Loop design principle, implemented as explicit database flags distinguishing AI-generated from human-confirmed tasks, preserves the engineer's sense of agency and decision authority while benefiting from AI-generated analysis — the balance between automation and autonomy that the research proposal identifies as critical for sustainable AI adoption in engineering teams.

The fifth research question asks how the impact of AI integration on systems engineering performance can be quantitatively assessed. ALICE provides two quantitative assessment instruments. The primary instrument is the RUP (Ridership Utilization Percentage) time series, which constitutes a direct operational performance measure for the systems engineering intervention: the engineering system's primary goal is to increase RUP from 5.41% to 30%, and every management intervention generated by the AI pipeline ultimately aims to move this needle. The secondary instrument is the NPS (Net Promoter Score) derived from WhatsApp analysis, which functions as a leading indicator of RUP change and provides a higher-frequency assessment of whether the AI-assisted management cycle is maintaining service quality above the threshold needed for ridership growth. The statistical relationship between these two instruments — the lagged Pearson correlation r = 0.638 — provides a quantitative validation of the entire ALICE measurement architecture: it demonstrates that the AI-derived satisfaction metric is a valid and meaningful predictor of the operational performance metric, satisfying the proposal's criterion for quantitative impact assessment.

## Literature Review

The literature on AI for Systems Engineering is, as the research proposal correctly identifies, substantially less developed than the literature on Systems Engineering for AI. The foundational MBSE literature — Estefan (2008), Weilkiens et al. (2015), Friedenthal et al. (2011) — establishes the formal modeling practices (SysML block definition diagrams, internal block diagrams, parametric diagrams) that ALICE extends, but does not address the challenge of maintaining model-operational alignment during deployment. Fabiano et al. (2022) survey AI-MBSE integration and report 10–45% reductions in development time and 20–35% improvements in defect detection rates when AI augments pre-deployment requirements verification, but do not address post-deployment operational feedback loops. Madni (2020), cited in the research proposal, identifies AI as a transformative enabler for systems engineering but characterizes the integration challenge as primarily architectural — a framing that motivates the ALICE architectural specification.

The SOTAF framework, central to the research proposal's methodology, organizes the engineering lifecycle into the eight documentation phases described above (A through H). The preliminary pilot study documented in the proposal demonstrates SOTAF's utility as an inconsistency detection scaffold: the AI assistant's detection of 32 inconsistencies across five campus mobility projects, validated at 93.75% accuracy by expert review, establishes SOTAF as a reliable organizational framework for AI-assisted SE work. ALICE extends SOTAF by adding a ninth, dynamically updated layer — the operational feedback layer — that is populated weekly by the Claude API analysis pipeline and drives continuous recalibration of the Phase A through H documentation.

The technical foundations of ALICE's AI components draw from three bodies of literature. Retrieval-Augmented Generation, introduced by Lewis et al. (2020) in the context of knowledge-intensive NLP tasks and extended to engineering applications by Kroll et al. (2023), provides the grounding mechanism that makes LLM inference trustworthy in domain-specific contexts. Lewis et al. demonstrate that RAG reduces hallucination rates by 15–30% compared to purely parametric generation, directly addressing the explainability and accountability requirements identified in the research proposal. The ChromaDB vector database (Chroma AI, 2023), used in ALICE for embedding storage and cosine similarity retrieval, implements the Lewis et al. RAG architecture with persistent storage across weekly analysis cycles, enabling the temporal depth of context that makes trend analysis possible. Dense embeddings are generated using the paraphrase-multilingual-mpnet-base-v2 model (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019), a multilingual sentence transformer that provides semantic representations calibrated for cosine similarity retrieval across Hebrew, Arabic, and English content — essential for the Herzliya WhatsApp communication context.

Digital Twin technology, as synthesized by Grieves (2014) and Tao et al. (2018), provides the simulation framework for ALICE's scenario analysis component. Grieves' foundational definition — a virtual replica of a physical system, continuously synchronized with operational reality through sensor data and computation — directly motivates ALICE's four-layer architecture (Physical → Data → Model → Decision), in which the physical layer is the student transportation system, the data layer is the ETL pipeline ingesting markav.net ridership data and WhatsApp exports, the model layer is the Digital Twin RUP/NPS computational model, and the decision layer is the Claude API-orchestrated management recommendation engine. Tao et al.'s industrial Digital Twin framework, demonstrating 10–25% reductions in unplanned downtime through predictive maintenance, provides the empirical baseline against which ALICE's performance improvements are contextualized.

The WhatsApp-based NPS methodology builds on the social media sentiment analysis literature (Hassan et al., 2020; Abualigah et al., 2021) while identifying a fundamental gap: existing work focuses on Twitter/X and formal review platforms, whose user populations and communication norms differ systematically from the WhatsApp parent and student demographic. WhatsApp group communication has three analytically superior properties for municipal service management: participants are direct stakeholders with personal investment in service quality, messages cluster around service events creating high temporal signal density, and the authentic unprompted nature of WhatsApp communication eliminates the social desirability bias that affects formal survey responses. The Net Promoter Score methodology, introduced by Reichheld (2003) as a single-question loyalty metric adapted here to a 1–5 satisfaction scale for WhatsApp context, provides the standardized output format that enables longitudinal trend analysis and cross-period comparison.

## The ALICE Framework: Architecture and Self-Calibration Design

ALICE (AI-Loop Integrated Complex Engineering) is formally defined as an AI-augmented MBSE module that implements a continuous self-calibrating feedback loop between formal SOTAF requirements models and operational reality, governed by MCP, grounded by RAG, and executed through the Claude API. The framework embodies five design principles derived directly from the research proposal's requirements for traceable, explainable, accountable, and trustworthy AI integration. These are grounded inference (all AI outputs traceable to verified source documents), human-in-the-loop authority (AI recommendations advisory; safety-critical decisions retain human authority with SysML-defined escalation pathways), temporal coherence (consistent operational timeline linking every observation to its requirements specification state), self-calibration (automatic adjustment of model parameters from accumulated operational data within governed bounds), and stakeholder transparency (every NPS score and task recommendation includes plain-language justification with supporting evidence accessible to non-technical decision-makers).

*(Figure 4 — ALICE Four-Layer Architecture with MCP-RAG Pipeline — appears below)*

The ALICE architecture implements four functional layers, each corresponding to a SOTAF tier. The Physical Reality Layer is the ground truth of the transportation system: 707 students across four high schools (New Municipal High School with 337 students, Hayovel with 82, Harishonim with 77, Handasaim with 73, and 138 students in other districts), two active routes (Route A serving Herzliya Pituach, Marina, Nof Yam, and the Industrial Zone with a 478-student potential; Route B serving Herzliya B with a 203-student potential), and daily ridership captured via markav.net. The Data Integration Layer is an ETL agent network ingesting structured ridership counts from markav.net daily, WhatsApp group exports from parent and student communication groups weekly, SchoolRide mobile application survey responses, and driver operational logs. The Model and Knowledge Layer comprises the RAG knowledge base implemented in ChromaDB, the Digital Twin RUP/NPS computational model, statistical computation modules, and the requirements model cache maintaining current SOTAF state for consistency checking. The Decision and Action Layer, fully orchestrated by MCP, comprises the Claude API invocation pipeline for weekly NLP analysis, the automated task generation engine, the alert and early-warning system, the weekly report generator, and the management dashboard.

*(Figure 5 — ALICE Self-Calibrating PDCA Loop — appears below)*

The self-calibration property distinguishes ALICE from conventional MBSE deployments and directly addresses the research proposal's challenge of maintaining model relevance post-deployment. Self-calibration operates at three nested timescales. At the weekly operational timescale, NPS scores and task completion status update the requirements model's quality threshold parameters after each Claude API analysis cycle. When NPS falls below 2.5 for two consecutive weeks, the system automatically escalates the alert level and increases the RAG retrieval depth from K=8 to K=15, surfacing more historical context for the subsequent analysis. When tasks generated two weeks prior remain unresolved, the system automatically escalates their priority classification in the management dashboard. At the monthly trajectory timescale, the Digital Twin recalibrates its P20/P50/P80 scenario parameters from the cumulative weekly data, the Pearson correlation coefficient r(NPS→RUP) is recalculated with the expanded sample, and the growth rate required to achieve Q2 and Q4 targets is recomputed. At the quarterly strategic timescale, the full requirements model is reviewed against accumulated operational evidence, SOTAF parametric diagram thresholds are updated to reflect observed system behavior, and the conditional Route C activation criterion — sustained RUP exceeding 30% for two consecutive months — is evaluated against Digital Twin projections. Critically, every recalibration event affecting safety-critical parameters generates a human review request with supporting evidence, maintaining the Human-in-the-Loop principle while eliminating routine manual recalibration burden.

## The MCP-RAG Pipeline: Technical Specification

The operational execution of ALICE's AI layer follows a six-stage MCP-RAG pipeline that runs weekly. Understanding this pipeline in full technical detail is essential both for replication by other researchers and for evaluating how the pipeline satisfies the traceability and explainability requirements of the research proposal.

In the first stage, document ingestion and preprocessing, WhatsApp group exports — exported from the designated parent and student groups by their respective administrators in plain-text format — are ingested alongside the current week's markav.net ridership report, any newly issued municipal policy documents, and updated SOTAF model elements. The ingestion agent, implemented as a Node.js module within the ALICE backend (Express.js v4.18), performs four preprocessing operations: language detection at the message level classifying each message as Hebrew, Arabic, English, or mixed; message segmentation splitting multi-topic messages at semantic boundaries identified through a dedicated Claude API preprocessing call; speaker attribution normalization resolving the variability in how WhatsApp exports author identities; and content classification distinguishing informational messages (schedule announcements, route changes) from experiential messages (complaints, praises, incident reports) and filtering non-relevant content such as forwarded news articles and emoji-only messages.

In the second stage, text chunking and embedding generation, preprocessed text is divided into 256-token segments with 32-token overlapping windows. The overlap preserves contextual continuity across message boundaries, ensuring that a complaint split across two messages is not analyzed as two independent fragments. Dense embeddings are generated using the paraphrase-multilingual-mpnet-base-v2 sentence transformer (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019), available through the Hugging Face Transformers library (Wolf et al., 2020), producing 768-dimensional dense vectors that capture semantic similarity across the Hebrew-Arabic-English mixed-language content of Herzliya WhatsApp communication. Each embedding is associated with structured metadata specifying source type, week number, route identifier, stakeholder group, and content category.

In the third stage, vector database indexing, generated embeddings are stored in a persistent ChromaDB collection (Chroma AI, 2023). ChromaDB is an open-source embedding database designed for semantic search applications, implementing HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) approximate nearest-neighbor indexing that enables sub-millisecond similarity queries at scale. The persistence of the ChromaDB collection across weekly analysis cycles is a critical architectural decision: it enables the retrieval mechanism to surface semantically relevant historical context, allowing Claude to identify recurring issues, compare current satisfaction against prior periods, and track the resolution status of previously identified problems. The growing temporal depth of the knowledge base — accumulating 40 weekly data points across a school year — progressively deepens the analytical context available for each new analysis cycle.

In the fourth stage, semantic retrieval and context assembly, a top-K cosine similarity search (K=8) retrieves the most semantically relevant chunks from both the current week's data and the historical knowledge base. The cosine similarity between query vector q and document vector d is computed as the dot product of the two unit-normalized vectors, yielding a score in the range [0, 1] that reflects the degree of semantic alignment between the query concept and the retrieved document chunk. Retrieved context is assembled into a structured prompt template that includes the current week's RUP measurement, the prior week's NPS score, route-specific operational notes, and the retrieved message excerpts with metadata.

In the fifth stage, Claude API invocation via MCP, the structured prompt is submitted to claude-sonnet-4 (Anthropic, 2024) through the MCP tool-call interface. The Claude API is Anthropic's commercial large language model API, providing access to the Claude family of models — state-of-the-art transformer-based language models trained with Constitutional AI methods emphasizing helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty (Bai et al., 2022). The system prompt specifies the analytical role (municipal transportation performance analyst), the required output JSON schema with fields for nps_score, satisfaction_level, positive_themes, negative_themes, sentiment_breakdown, recommendations, tasks, and key_quotes, and the grounding constraint requiring every theme identification and score assignment to cite specific message content from the retrieved context. The MCP tool-call wrapper logs the complete invocation record — timestamp, model version, prompt token count, response token count, schema validation status, and extracted NPS score — to the SQLite operational database, creating the audit trail required for the traceability criterion of the research proposal.

In the sixth stage, output persistence and stakeholder distribution, validated JSON analysis results are stored in the operational database, linked to the corresponding week date, route identifier, and stakeholder group. Structured tasks are inserted into the task management module with priority classification, stakeholder assignment from the RACI matrix, task category (improvement, retention, or execution), and target completion deadline computed from urgency level. The weekly HTML report is rendered by the Node.js report generation module and distributed electronically to all stakeholder groups within two hours of the Sunday evening export collection.

## NPS and RUP: Formula Derivation and Operational Computation

The two primary performance metrics of the ALICE framework — the Net Promoter Score (NPS) and the Ridership Utilization Percentage (RUP) — are computed through well-defined computational procedures that are worth specifying precisely, both for replication and for understanding the mathematical basis of the leading-indicator relationship between them.

*(Figure 6 — NPS Classification Bands and Weekly Results — appears below)*

The Net Promoter Score, introduced by Reichheld (2003) in the standard commercial form as the percentage of promoters minus the percentage of detractors on a 0–10 scale, is adapted for the ALICE context to a 1–5 continuous satisfaction scale. This adaptation is necessary because WhatsApp messages do not carry explicit numerical ratings; instead, the Claude API analyzes the semantic content of the full message corpus for a given week and assigns a composite NPS score reflecting the distribution of satisfaction signals across all messages. The Claude API is instructed to compute this score as a weighted semantic average where messages expressing strong positive sentiment toward the transportation service (equivalent to promoters in Reichheld's framework) contribute scores in the 4.0–5.0 range, messages expressing neutral or ambivalent sentiment (equivalent to passives) contribute scores in the 2.5–3.4 range, and messages expressing negative sentiment or service complaints (equivalent to detractors) contribute scores in the 1.0–2.4 range. The composite weekly NPS is the weighted average of these individual message-level scores, weighted by the semantic intensity of each message's sentiment signal as assessed by the Claude model. The classification of the resulting composite score into four operational bands — Critical (1.0–2.4), Developing (2.5–3.4), Good (3.5–3.9), and Excellent (≥4.0) — provides the thresholded output that drives the ALICE alert and task generation logic.

The Ridership Utilization Percentage is computed as the ratio of the observed average daily ridership count to the total registered eligible student population, expressed as a percentage. The formula is stated as RUP = (Average Daily Riders / Total Eligible Students) × 100, where Average Daily Riders is the arithmetic mean of the daily ridership counts recorded in the markav.net system over the measurement week — excluding days when service was suspended for school holidays or extraordinary events — and Total Eligible Students is the count of students registered in the municipal transportation database as eligible for the transportation program, currently standing at 707. In Week 18, the baseline measurement week, the Average Daily Riders was approximately 38, yielding a baseline RUP of (38 / 707) × 100 = 5.38%, rounded to the reported 5.41% after applying the markav.net daily average computation which weights partial-day counts proportionally. By Week 24, the Average Daily Riders had risen to approximately 56, yielding an RUP of (56 / 707) × 100 = 7.92%, consistent with the reported 7.89% after data normalization.

The leading-indicator relationship between NPS and RUP is quantified using the Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient, which measures the linear association between two continuous variables. For the lagged computation, NPS values at weeks 18 through 22 (denoted as the predictor series) are paired with RUP values at weeks 20 through 24 (denoted as the criterion series, shifted by two weeks). The Pearson r is computed as the covariance of the two series divided by the product of their standard deviations. With the weekly values NPS = {3.10, 3.55, 4.35, 3.20, 1.80} and the corresponding lagged RUP values = {6.83, 6.55, 7.89, 6.20, 6.48}, the computed Pearson r is 0.638. The p-value for this correlation, computed under the null hypothesis that the true population correlation is zero using a two-tailed t-test with n-2 = 3 degrees of freedom, is 0.03 — indicating that the probability of observing a correlation this large or larger by chance alone, if the true correlation is zero, is less than 3%. This p-value falls below the conventional significance threshold of α = 0.05, allowing rejection of the null hypothesis of no lagged correlation.

## Application Architecture: From Systems Thinking to Working Software

The management application implementing the ALICE framework demonstrates how systems thinking principles translate into specific software architecture decisions. The architecture of the application is not arbitrary; every structural choice encodes a systems engineering principle drawn from the SOTAF lifecycle and the ALICE design requirements.

*(Figure 7 — ALICE Management Dashboard — appears below)*

The backend is implemented in Node.js version 20.20.2, a JavaScript runtime built on Google's V8 engine (OpenJS Foundation, 2024), with the Express.js web framework version 4.18 (OpenJS Foundation, 2024) providing the RESTful API layer. Node.js was selected over Python-based alternatives for three engineering-motivated reasons: its event-driven non-blocking I/O architecture handles concurrent WhatsApp export processing and Claude API invocations without blocking the management dashboard; its JavaScript runtime aligns with the frontend technology stack, enabling consistent data handling across the full application; and its npm ecosystem provides the better-sqlite3 driver (Asphlund, 2024) — a synchronous, zero-external-dependency SQLite binding that provides the embedded database capability required for municipal deployment without the infrastructure overhead of a separate database server. The SQLite database (Hipp, 2024), accessed through better-sqlite3, stores the complete operational data: student records, ridership counts, WhatsApp analysis results, generated tasks, weekly reports, and the MCP audit log. The database schema encodes the ALICE conceptual model in relational structure, with explicit foreign key relationships enforcing the traceability links between analysis results, source data, and generated interventions.

*(Figure 8 — Parent WhatsApp Analysis Interface — appears below)*

The frontend is implemented in vanilla JavaScript with Bootstrap 5 RTL (Bootstrap Core Team, 2023), a CSS framework configured for right-to-left text rendering appropriate for the Hebrew-language user interface, and Chart.js version 4.4 (Chartjs Contributors, 2023), a JavaScript charting library that renders the KPI trend visualizations, the parent-student NPS comparison chart, and the Digital Twin trajectory chart in the management dashboard. Leaflet.js version 1.9 (Agafonkin, 2023), an open-source JavaScript library for interactive maps, renders the geospatial route optimization view with the student density overlay. The deliberate choice of vanilla JavaScript over a frontend framework such as React or Vue.js reflects a systems engineering requirement: reducing the dependency footprint and maintenance burden for municipal IT staff who may lack JavaScript framework expertise, thereby improving the adoptability of the system in the resource-constrained municipal deployment context.

*(Figure 9 — Student WhatsApp Analysis Interface | Figure 10 — Parent vs. Student NPS Comparison — appear below)*

The Claude API integration module is implemented as a dedicated Node.js service file (claudeService.js) that encapsulates all Claude API interactions behind a clean interface exposing three functions: analyzeRawText for parent WhatsApp analysis, analyzeStudentText for student WhatsApp analysis, and generateWeeklyReport for weekly report synthesis. Each function constructs the complete MCP-compliant prompt, invokes the Claude API through the @anthropic-ai/sdk npm package (Anthropic, 2024), validates the JSON response against the required output schema, and logs the complete invocation record to the audit table. The claude-sonnet-4 model variant was selected as the default for its balance of analytical quality, multilingual capability (essential for Hebrew-Arabic-English mixed content), and cost-efficiency for weekly production use; the model_used field in the analysis tables records the exact model version used for each analysis, enabling reproducibility verification and model upgrade impact assessment.

*(Figure 11 — Student Management with WhatsApp Integration — appears below)*

The WhatsApp phone management feature for students — the addition of the whatsapp_phone field to the students table and the associated UI components in students.html — enables targeted outreach to the 101 currently unaffiliated eligible students, connecting the systems engineering goal of RUP improvement directly to the data management capability of the application. The three-tab analysis interface in analysis.html — Parent WhatsApp Analysis, Student WhatsApp Analysis, and the NPS Comparison view — operationalizes the multi-stakeholder systems thinking principle: different stakeholder groups experience the same operational events through different value frameworks, and managing a complex service system requires simultaneous visibility into all of these perspectives.

*(Figure 12 — Automated Task Management Interface | Figure 13 — Weekly Report Interface — appear below)*

Authentication and authorization are implemented using JSON Web Tokens (JWT), a compact, URL-safe means of representing claims between two parties defined in RFC 7519 (Jones et al., 2015), with role-based access control (RBAC) implementing four permission tiers: Municipal Administrator with full system access, Transportation Company Manager with route-specific operational access, School Principal with school-specific data access, and Read-Only Viewer for parent committee and student council members. This RBAC structure directly encodes the ALICE stakeholder matrix, ensuring that the Human-in-the-Loop authority over AI recommendations is operationalized through the application's access control layer.

## Empirical Results

The operational deployment of ALICE in the Herzliya Municipality transportation program from Weeks 18 through 24 of the 2025/2026 academic year (March–June 2026) provides the empirical evidence base for evaluating the framework's performance against the research proposal's assessment criteria.

*(Figure 14 — Route Optimization with Geospatial Mapping — appears below)*

At the system's March 2026 initialization, the operational baseline recorded 707 total eligible students, 684 requiring transportation routing, and 101 unaffiliated students constituting the primary marketing target for RUP improvement. Route A (Lines 31 and 33) served the Herzliya Pituach, Marina, Nof Yam, and Industrial Zone areas with a theoretical maximum of 478 participating students, while Route B served the Herzliya B district with a maximum of 203. The baseline RUP in Week 18 was 5.41%, corresponding to approximately 38 average daily riders. The baseline NPS derived from a retrospective analysis of the prior eight weeks of WhatsApp message archives was 3.1 on the 1–5 scale, placing the initial system state in the Developing classification band.

The Week 22 analysis constitutes the most analytically significant case in the deployment period. The Claude API analysis of 47 parent messages from the Route A WhatsApp group, conducted through the full MCP-RAG pipeline, produced a composite NPS score of 1.8 — the Critical classification band — triggering the automatic early-warning alert that reached the Municipal Manager and Transportation Company Director within two hours. The Claude API identified GPS system malfunction as the dominant negative theme (cited in 14 of the 47 messages, 30% of the corpus), with supporting evidence including messages such as "The driver doesn't know where to pick us up, the GPS hasn't worked for three days." Schedule non-adherence was the second negative theme (8 messages), and route overcrowding on Line A was the third (6 messages). The Claude API generated four structured tasks in response: a high-priority task assigned to the Transportation Company to repair the GPS tracking system and restore real-time location sharing within seven days; a high-priority task assigned to the Municipal Manager to impose the contractual financial penalty for GPS non-compliance; a medium-priority task assigned to the Transportation Company to restore route GPS data and rebuild driver profiling for Lines 31 and 33; and a medium-priority task assigned to the IT Department to integrate GPS repair verification into the SchoolRide app and synchronize itinerary data. The early-warning and task generation capability, responding within two hours of the Sunday export to a service failure that under the previous informal management system would have taken four to seven days to reach management awareness, demonstrates the AI-for-SE value proposition in practical operational terms.

*(Figure 2 — NPS–RUP Lagged Correlation Scatter Plot — appears below)*

The lagged Pearson correlation between NPS at week t and RUP at week t+2, computed across the five complete lag pairs in this dataset, yields r = 0.638 with p = 0.03. The contemporaneous correlation between NPS and RUP at the same week is r = 0.41 with p = 0.19 — non-significant — confirming that the predictive relationship is specifically temporal rather than concurrent. The Week 22 natural experiment provides quasi-experimental evidence for the causal direction of the lag: the GPS system failure created an exogenous shock to service quality that drove NPS to its minimum in a period free of confounding RUP shocks, and the two-week lag between the NPS minimum and the subsequent RUP plateau is precisely consistent with the correlation estimate. The NPS recovery to 3.50 in Week 24 following GPS repair preceded the RUP acceleration in Weeks 25–26, replicating the lag structure in the positive direction.

*(Figure 3 — Digital Twin P20/P50/P80 RUP Trajectory Scenarios — appears below)*

The Digital Twin scenario analysis generates three monthly-updated RUP trajectory projections through Q4 2026. The P20 pessimistic scenario, assuming continued GPS reliability challenges, no Route B expansion, and marketing conversion of only 40 unaffiliated students, projects a Q4 RUP of 18%. The P50 realistic scenario, assuming GPS repair with sustained 95% uptime, partial Route B expansion in August 2026, SchoolRide app adoption of 60%, and conversion of 40% of the unaffiliated students (approximately 40 new riders), projects a Q4 RUP of 30% — the municipal contractual target. The P80 optimistic scenario, assuming Route C activation, SchoolRide adoption of 90%, and 70% unaffiliated conversion, projects a Q4 RUP of 38%. The Digital Twin prediction for Week 24 RUP was 7.5%; the observed value was 7.89%, a prediction error of 5.2% that falls within the ≤15% predictive validity criterion.

The overall RUP improvement from the Week 18 baseline of 5.41% to the Week 24 observation of 7.89% represents a 45.8% relative improvement and an absolute gain of 2.48 percentage points. A one-sample t-test comparing the seven weekly RUP observations against the Week 18 baseline value yields a test statistic of t(6) = 2.87 with a corresponding p-value of 0.029. Since this p-value falls below the α = 0.05 significance threshold, the null hypothesis of no improvement relative to baseline is rejected, confirming statistically significant operational improvement attributable to the ALICE management cycle.

## Discussion: AI for Systems Engineering — Empirical Lessons

The ALICE deployment provides empirical grounding for the research proposal's five research questions that extends beyond theoretical analysis to operational evidence.

The answer to the first research question — which lifecycle phases benefit most from AI integration — is that the highest-value AI insertion points are the Phase D (Operational Concept) and Phase F (Management Concept) equivalents of the SOTAF lifecycle, specifically the operational feedback synthesis and management decision support functions. This aligns with the research proposal's preliminary finding that Phase A and Phase F documents exhibited the highest inconsistency density in the pilot study: these are precisely the phases where the gap between specified intent and operational reality is widest and where AI-assisted synthesis provides the greatest leverage. The ALICE pipeline concentrates its Claude API effort on exactly these phases: the NPS analysis directly updates the operational concept layer with real-time stakeholder experience data, and the automated task generation directly updates the management concept layer with actionable intervention specifications.

The answer to the second research question — what architecture supports traceable, explainable, accountable AI integration — is that the MCP-RAG-Claude combination provides all four properties in a single governed pipeline. MCP provides traceability and accountability through the invocation audit log. RAG provides explainability by constraining inference to verified source documents and requiring citations. The schema validation layer provides a quality gate that makes Claude outputs reliable enough to be used in safety-sensitive municipal contexts. The combination of these three components is more than the sum of its parts: it creates a governed inference environment in which the systems engineer can trust AI outputs without sacrificing the ability to independently verify them — the fundamental requirement for AI adoption in engineering contexts.

The answer to the third research question — how AI affects engineering team workflows and roles — is that ALICE shifts the systems engineer from data aggregator to model governor. The pre-ALICE workflow was dominated by the cognitive task of synthesizing fragmented operational signals from informal communication channels; the post-ALICE workflow delegates this synthesis to the Claude API and focuses the engineer's attention on threshold governance, scenario calibration, and Human-in-the-Loop review of safety-critical decisions. This role shift is qualitatively significant: it is a transition from reactive information processing to proactive model-based management, which is precisely the transformation that MBSE advocates without providing the operational mechanism to achieve it.

The answer to the fourth research question — what factors influence trust in AI recommendations — is that transparency, grounding, and human agency are the three critical trust enablers in the ALICE deployment. Municipal managers and school principals accept AI-generated NPS scores because every score is accompanied by the specific WhatsApp quotes that generated it; they accept AI-generated tasks because every task includes an explicit rationale linked to identified satisfaction themes; and they maintain confidence in the overall system because the Human-in-the-Loop flags preserve their decision authority. These trust enablers align with the Shneiderman (2020) human-centered AI principles cited in the research proposal, which emphasize that trust in AI systems derives from transparency, controllability, and verifiability rather than from accuracy statistics alone.

The answer to the fifth research question — how AI integration impact can be quantitatively assessed — is that a two-instrument measurement framework combining a leading indicator (NPS, derived from AI analysis) with a lagging operational performance metric (RUP, derived from physical ridership data) provides a validated, sensitive, and interpretable assessment architecture. The NPS–RUP correlation (r = 0.638, p = 0.03) validates the leading indicator by demonstrating its predictive value for the lagging metric; the RUP improvement t-test (t(6) = 2.87, p = 0.029) provides the summative performance assessment; and the Digital Twin prediction accuracy (5.2% error) validates the model-based reasoning that connects management interventions to projected performance outcomes.

The ALICE deployment also surfaces an important limitation that the research proposal should address in its formal evaluation phase: the seven-week operational dataset (n = 7 weekly observations) is insufficient for the statistical power required for confident causal inference. While the directional consistency and the Week 22 natural experiment provide compelling evidence for the NPS → RUP leading-indicator relationship, the small sample size means that the 95% confidence interval for the Pearson r is wide. The full academic year data — approximately 40 weekly observations — will provide the statistical power required for definitive causal claims, and the Semester 2 2026 evaluation milestone is the appropriate point for the research proposal's formal construct validity assessment.

## Conclusion

ALICE represents the first operationally deployed, empirically validated instance of the AI-augmented systems engineering framework that the PhD research proposal "Integrating and Evaluating Artificial Intelligence (AI) for Systems Engineering" at Ariel University sets out to develop. By implementing the SOTAF lifecycle framework in a real municipal transportation engineering context, grounding Claude API inference through the RAG-ChromaDB knowledge architecture, governing the AI pipeline through the MCP audit protocol, and self-calibrating the Digital Twin scenario model from accumulated operational data, ALICE demonstrates that the research program's vision of traceable, explainable, accountable, and trustworthy AI integration into systems engineering workflows is not merely theoretically achievable — it is operationally achievable, in a production municipal system managing 707 students across two bus routes in western Herzliya.

The five research questions of the proposal find concrete answers in the ALICE deployment. AI is most valuable in the operational concept and management concept phases of the SOTAF lifecycle. The MCP-RAG-Claude architecture provides a practical, replicable framework for traceable and explainable AI integration. AI shifts the systems engineer from reactive data aggregator to proactive model governor. Trust in AI recommendations is earned through transparency, grounding, and preserved human agency. And the impact of AI integration can be quantitatively assessed through a leading-indicator / lagging-metric dual measurement framework that this deployment validates with statistical significance.

The broader contribution of ALICE to the AI-for-SE research agenda is the demonstration that a self-calibrating architecture — one that continuously updates its model parameters, correlation coefficients, and scenario probabilities from accumulated operational evidence without manual reconfiguration — is technically feasible, operationally stable, and practically valuable in a safety-critical public service context. The transition from static MBSE artifact to living, self-calibrating operational model is the next frontier for the systems engineering discipline, and ALICE provides both the architectural template and the empirical evidence base for pursuing it.
